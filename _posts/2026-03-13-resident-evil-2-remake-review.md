## Overview

Resident Evil 2 Remake is the rare case where a reimagining doesn't just honor the original — it surpasses it. Capcom took a 1998 survival horror classic, stripped it to its mechanical skeleton, rebuilt it in a modern third-person perspective, and delivered what is arguably the most polished horror game of the past decade.

What makes RE2R remarkable isn't just the graphical overhaul. It's the way Capcom preserved the feel of the original — the claustrophobia, the resource anxiety, the dread of the Raccoon City Police Department's dark corridors — while making every system feel contemporary and intentional.

Leon S. Kennedy and Claire Redfield's campaigns offer two overlapping perspectives on the same nightmare. The RPD is a puzzle box, and the second playthrough recontextualizes everything you thought you understood about the first.

---

## 🎨 Game Design & Mechanics — 9.5 / 10

The core game loop is built around resource scarcity as emotional pressure. Ammunition is limited but not scarce enough to halt progress — the game is calibrated so that careful players always have enough, while careless ones feel the squeeze.

The Raccoon City Police Department is one of the best-designed game environments in recent memory. It functions as a metroidvania-style space — rooms lock and unlock as key items are found, shortcuts reveal themselves, and the map becomes legible only after you've suffered through it.

Mr. X — the Tyrant — is the game's most brilliant design addition. As a persistent pursuer who patrols the RPD in real time, he converts the entire building into a hostile space, creating emergent gameplay moments that no scripted encounter could replicate.

---

## 🖼️ Graphics & Art Direction — 9.4 / 10

RE2R runs on Capcom's RE Engine, delivering extraordinary environmental detail — peeling paint, water-damaged floors, blood smears that catch the light. Lighting is the standout technical achievement: Leon's flashlight creates dynamic shadows, while Claire's environments shift toward warmer tones that reflect her narrative arc.

---

## 🎵 Music & Sound Design — 9.6 / 10

The sound design is exceptional. The score uses ambient industrial noise and sudden silences far more effectively than jump scare stingers. Mr. X's footsteps — audible through walls, rhythmically irregular — generate hours of sustained dread through procedural audio design alone.

---

## 📖 Story & World-Building — 8.8 / 10

RE2R's narrative is deliberately B-movie in tone — and better for it. The dual-campaign structure is the greatest strength: events overlap, characters miss each other by minutes, and the full picture only emerges when both campaigns are complete. The sewers and NEST lab sections dip in pacing, but the RPD half is near-perfect.

---

## ⚙️ Controls & Feel — 8.7 / 10

The over-the-shoulder controls feel weighty and deliberate — Leon moves like a man in genuine danger. Aiming has satisfying physicality, with visible hit reactions and zombie body damage that tracks across encounters.

---

## 💡 Game Design Student's Take

RE2R offers two essential design lessons.

**Persistent enemies as spatial design tools**: Mr. X restructures the entire game space. Every room is evaluated not just by its contents but by its exit options. One enemy — entire building becomes a new game.

**The remake as design critique**: Capcom identified what made the original work at a systemic level and preserved it while updating the surface. Study the differences between RE2 1998 and RE2R 2019 — the delta is a lesson in design translation.

If I were to redesign one element: the second-half environments. The sewers abandon the RPD's metroidvania logic for linear progression, losing the spatial mastery the first half builds so carefully.
